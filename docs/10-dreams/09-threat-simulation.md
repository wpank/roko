## Threat Simulation Theory

Revonsuo (2000, Behavioral and Brain Sciences, "The reinterpretation of dreams: An evolutionary hypothesis of the function of dreaming") proposed that biological dreaming evolved primarily as a **threat rehearsal mechanism**. During sleep, the brain simulates threatening scenarios, rehearses responses, and strengthens threat-detection circuits — all without real-world consequences.

Key findings from the Threat Simulation Theory (TST):

1. Threat-related dream content is over-represented relative to waking experience (Revonsuo & Valli 2000)
2. Dream threats are often realistic and rehearse appropriate avoidance behaviors
3. The threat simulation function is most active following threatening waking experiences
4. Children in dangerous environments have more threat-focused dreams than children in safe environments

In Roko, threat simulation is implemented as a specialized REM imagination mode that deliberately constructs adversarial scenarios and rehearses the agent's responses.

## Adversarial Dreaming in Roko

Adversarial dreaming operates as a sub-mode of REM imagination. It fires when any of the following conditions are met:

1. **Recent failure**: The agent experienced a gate failure in the most recent Gamma cycle
2. **Novelty detection**: The agent encountered an unprecedented situation (low HDC similarity to any existing episode)
3. **Scheduled**: Every Nth dream cycle (configurable, default: every 3rd cycle), a portion of the REM phase is dedicated to adversarial dreaming regardless of recent events

### Three-Tier Threat Taxonomy

The adversarial dreaming engine categorizes threats into three tiers:

| Tier | Description | Example (Coding Agent) | Example (Research Agent) |
|------|-------------|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Tier 1: Known Threats** | Threats the agent has experienced before | Compile errors from type mismatches | Missing citations in literature reviews |
| **Tier 2: Anticipated Threats** | Threats the agent has not experienced but can infer from existing knowledge | Breaking changes in dependency updates | Conflicting findings across papers |
| **Tier 3: Novel Threats** | Threats generated by creative recombination of existing threat patterns | Simultaneous test failure + dependency conflict + CI timeout | Systematic bias in a widely-cited meta-analysis |

### Threat Generation Process

For each tier, the adversarial dreaming engine generates scenarios:

**Tier 1 (Known Threats)**:
```
You have experienced these failures in past episodes:
{failure_episodes}

For each failure type, construct a scenario where it happens AGAIN but
in a slightly different context. What early warning signs would you
watch for? What would you do differently?
```

**Tier 2 (Anticipated Threats)**:
```
Based on your current knowledge:
{relevant_heuristics}

What threats have you NOT yet encountered but could plausibly occur?
Consider: failure modes of your current strategies, edge cases of your
heuristics, scenarios where your assumptions break down.
```

**Tier 3 (Novel Threats)**:
Uses the same combinational creativity framework as REM imagination (see [03-rem-imagination.md](03-rem-imagination.md)), but directed specifically at threat scenarios:
```
Combine elements from these two unrelated failure episodes to imagine
a compound failure scenario that would be especially challenging:
Episode A (failure type: {type_a}): {summary_a}
Episode B (failure type: {type_b}): {summary_b}

What happens when BOTH failure types occur simultaneously?
What cascading effects would result?
```

### Threat Response Rehearsal

For each generated threat scenario, the agent rehearses its response:

```
Threat scenario: {threat_description}

1. What are the earliest detectable warning signs?
2. What is your immediate response (first 5 seconds)?
3. What is your recovery strategy if the immediate response fails?
4. What heuristic should be added to prevent this scenario?
```

Rehearsed responses enter the staging buffer as `Warning`-type hypotheses at confidence 0.25. If a similar threat actually occurs during waking and the rehearsed response proves effective, the warning is promoted to permanent knowledge.

## Gap Analysis

The adversarial dreaming engine also performs a **gap analysis** — identifying areas where the agent's threat coverage is thin:

1. Cluster all known failure episodes by HDC similarity
2. Identify regions of the failure space with no nearby episodes (empty clusters)
3. Generate hypothetical threats that would occupy those empty regions
4. Prioritize threat rehearsal for the least-covered regions

This ensures the agent's threat preparedness is balanced across its operational space, not concentrated around familiar failure modes.

## Regime-Aware Scheduling

Threat simulation intensity adapts to the agent's current operational context:

| Context | Threat Simulation Allocation | Rationale |
|---------|------------------------------|-----------|
| Normal operations, low error rate | 10% of REM phase | Maintenance-level vigilance |
| Recent failures detected | 30% of REM phase | Active threat learning |
| Novel environment detected (new task types, new tools) | 25% of REM phase | Anticipatory threat modeling |
| Post-crisis recovery | 40% of REM phase | Intensive threat rehearsal |

## Integration with Daimon

The Daimon's affect engine (see `../04-daimon/INDEX.md`) interacts with threat simulation in two ways:

1. **Threat salience**: Episodes with high negative arousal (fear, anxiety) are prioritized for threat simulation replay. The somatic marker hypothesis (Damasio 1994) predicts that emotionally tagged experiences are more readily retrieved and more thoroughly processed.

2. **Depotentiation of threat anxiety**: After threat simulation, the emotional charge of the rehearsed scenarios is reduced (Walker & van der Helm 2009). The agent has "practiced" its response to the threat, reducing the anxiety associated with it. This mirrors the therapeutic effect of controlled exposure in anxiety treatment.
