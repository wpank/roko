# 05 — Risks and Anti-Patterns

> Self-contained guide to what can go wrong and what not to do.
> Assumes no prior context. Read this before making architectural or product decisions.

**Date**: 2026-04-26
**Purpose**: Catalog every known failure mode, attack vector, regulatory cliff,
market trap, and anti-pattern from research, production incidents, and competitive
intelligence. Each section is self-contained with citations.

## 1. Technical Risks

### 1.1 Diversity Collapse at Scale

**The problem**: When N agents share a common base model, training corpus, and
prompting style, they converge on similar strategies. Independent viewpoints
collapse.

**Evidence**:
- Patel (April 2026): Mean cosine similarity 0.888 and effective rank 2.17 of
  3.0 in 100-question, 3-agent Qwen2.5-14B committees. Three agents provide
  roughly two independent viewpoints.
- Epistemic-diversity work measured Hill-Shannon claim diversity across 27 LLMs
  as far below rudimentary web search.
- "Verbalized Sampling" (2025) showed mode collapse is an inherent property of
  preference data, not just an RLHF artifact.
- OASIS empirically found LLM agents are *more* susceptible to herding than
  humans (replicating Muchnik et al. 2013's 116K-comment study).

**Operational impact**: Expect ~2-3x fewer effective independent viewpoints than
nominal agent count. A "10-agent committee" provides 3-5 effective viewpoints.
Homogeneous multi-agent debate reduces to majority vote in expectation.

**Mitigation**: Heterogeneous model selection (enforce different model families
per committee slot). HDC fingerprint diversity as a runtime constraint.
c-factor measurement gates evolutionary decisions to prevent diversity collapse.

### 1.2 Error Amplification in Multi-Agent Systems

**The problem**: Errors propagate and amplify through agent chains. A 3-agent
pipeline does not reduce error -- it can multiply it.

**Evidence**:
- Documented 17.2x error amplification in naive multi-agent systems where
  agents pass context without verification checkpoints.
- Princeton NLP found that a single well-tooled agent matches or outperforms
  multi-agent systems on 64% of tasks. Multi-agent is mostly architectural
  fashion at typical complexity levels.
- Enterprise multi-agent failure survey (Cemri/MAST taxonomy, March-April 2026):
  production failure rates of 41-86.7%, with 79% originating from coordination
  and spec issues, not model capability.

**Operational impact**: The binding constraint on enterprise deployment is
coordination, not model capability. Adding more agents to a failing pipeline
makes it fail faster and more expensively.

**Mitigation**: Verify at every handoff boundary (not just at pipeline end).
Pre-action `verify_pre()` vetoes before spending tokens. Gate each agent's
output independently.

### 1.3 DGM Reward Hacking

**The problem**: Any learnable reward signal can be exploited. An agent that
optimizes a gate metric will find ways to satisfy the metric without satisfying
the intent.

**Evidence**:
- Goodhart's Law applied to AI: weighted-sum verification is the most common
  failure mode. A sufficiently capable agent will maximize the weighted sum by
  exploiting the weakest criterion.
- DGM (deep generative model) reward hacking documented across RL and LLM
  fine-tuning settings.

**Mitigation**: Conjunctive hard criteria (AND gate -- all must pass, no
tradeoffs) + Pareto soft criteria (multi-objective, never weighted-sum). Evidence
typed separately from Criterion. Variance Inequality: verifier spectrally cleaner
than generator.

### 1.4 Model Collapse Under Synthetic Data

**The problem**: When models train on data generated by other models, quality
degrades exponentially across generations.

**Evidence**:
- Demonstrated across language models, image generators, and code models.
- The dream cycle (L3) generates synthetic knowledge from agent episodes.
  Without safeguards, the knowledge store can accumulate model artifacts
  (confident-sounding but wrong patterns).

**Mitigation**: L3 knowledge requires Verify passage for promotion. Tier
progression (Transient -> Working -> Consolidated) with increasing confirmation
thresholds. AntiKnowledge actively repels known-bad patterns in HDC space.
No knowledge reaches Persistent tier without human or consortium approval.

### 1.5 Benchmark Exploitation

**The problem**: Benchmarks are gamed. Published numbers do not reflect real
capability.

**Evidence**:
- SWE-bench Verified: OpenAI stopped reporting scores in early 2026 due to
  confirmed contamination. 59.4% of hardest unsolved problems had flawed test
  cases. SWE-bench Pro (Scale AI) is now the trusted benchmark.
- Cognition Devin's actual SWE-bench Verified resolution rate was 13.86%
  versus marketing implying autonomy. Cognition stopped reporting SWE-bench
  numbers entirely.
- HumanEval and MMLU are saturated; cost reductions measured on them are
  meaningless.

**Mitigation**: Use SWE-bench Pro for code tasks. Always report dual-axis
(cost AND accuracy). Replicate 3x with seed variation. Ablation studies for
any self-improvement claim. Without ablation, the claim is unfalsifiable.

### 1.6 Single-Agent Superiority

**The problem**: A single well-tooled agent beats multi-agent systems on the
majority of tasks. The multi-agent overhead is not justified for typical work.

**Evidence**:
- Princeton NLP: single agent matches or outperforms multi-agent on 64% of tasks.
- 3-agent pipelines burn ~3x single-agent tokens.
- Six recurring multi-agent failure modes at scale: infinite handoff loops,
  coordination overhead exceeding parallelism gains, token amplification, API
  rate-limit collisions, context loss across handoffs, single-orchestrator
  bottleneck at ~100 req/s.

**Mitigation**: Default to single-agent. Multi-agent only when the task
decomposes into genuinely independent sub-tasks with well-defined interfaces.
T0/T1/T2 gating routes most work to the cheapest sufficient tier.

## 2. Security Risks

### 2.1 Adaptive Attacks Against All Defenses

**The problem**: Every known LLM defense has been broken by adaptive attacks.
Detection-based defenses fail against adversaries who know the defense exists.

**Evidence**:
- Nasr et al. (2025): 90%+ adaptive attack ASR (attack success rate) against
  12 published defenses. The defenses tested include perplexity filtering,
  paraphrasing, instruction hierarchy, and system prompt protections.
- AutoInject: a 1.5B parameter model achieving 77.96% ASR for automated
  prompt injection. Attacks are now automatable at small model scale.

**Operational impact**: Prompt-based guardrails ("DON'T DO IT" repeated 11
times -- the Replit pattern) are language, not enforcement. Any defense that
operates at the prompt layer can be circumvented by an adversary who controls
part of the input.

**Mitigation**: CaMeL IFC (Information Flow Control) -- architectural, not
detection-based. Capability-tagged data flow where Extensions cannot launder
capabilities. The defense is structural: data tagged as `FsRead` cannot flow
to a `Net` operation regardless of what the prompt says. Detection is a backup,
not the primary mechanism.

### 2.2 Multi-Agent Security Gap (MASpi)

**The problem**: Single-agent defenses do not transfer to multi-agent settings.
When agents compose, the attack surface is the union of all agents' surfaces.

**Evidence**:
- MASpi (2025-2026): demonstrated that prompt injection defenses validated on
  single-agent benchmarks fail when the defended agent is part of a pipeline.
  The attacker targets the weakest agent in the chain, and the compromised
  output propagates through the pipeline.

**Mitigation**: Verify at every agent boundary, not just the pipeline boundary.
CaMeL tags propagate through the entire chain -- compromised data carries its
taint. RecursiveSafetyMonitor watches the full composition graph.

### 2.3 RAG Poisoning

**The problem**: Small amounts of crafted content in a knowledge store can
hijack agent behavior with high success rates.

**Evidence**:
- PoisonedRAG: 5 crafted text passages injected into a knowledge base achieved
  90% ASR. Standard RAG defenses (relevance filtering, source verification)
  had limited effect.
- Poisoned-MRAG (multimodal): 5 image-text pairs into a 481K-entry database
  achieved 98% attack success.

**Operational impact**: Any knowledge store that accepts external input is a
prompt injection surface. The neuro store, research artifacts, and marketplace
knowledge bundles are all potential targets.

**Mitigation**: Knowledge tier progression with verification gates. L3 knowledge
requires multiple independent confirmations before promotion. AntiKnowledge
actively blocks known-bad patterns. CaMeL taint tracking marks knowledge by
provenance -- external knowledge carries higher taint than internally generated.

### 2.4 Supply Chain Attacks

**The problem**: Agent toolchains are high-leverage attack targets because a
single compromised dependency runs in thousands of agent contexts.

**Evidence**:
- Cline npm publish compromise (2026): compromised package in the agent tool
  ecosystem.
- Mercor / LiteLLM supply-chain compromise (TechCrunch, late March 2026):
  shared agent-stack OSS as high-leverage attack target.
- Forcepoint Unit 42: documented 10 in-the-wild indirect prompt injection
  payloads including `rm -rf` against Claude Code/Cursor/Copilot and $5,000
  PayPal.me fraud payloads.

**Operational impact**: Indirect prompt injection is now operational threat,
not theoretical. Any MCP server, marketplace Cell, or external tool can carry
a payload.

**Mitigation**: Marketplace artifacts verified by checksum (blake3) and publisher
signature (Sigstore). WASM sandbox with fuel metering prevents arbitrary execution.
Static analysis on submitted WASM Cells. Three-layer capability intersection
ensures even a compromised Cell can only access declared resources. Script
Cells restricted to verified publishers on public marketplace.

### 2.5 Agent-Specific Attack Vectors

**The problem**: Agents introduce attack surfaces that static code does not have.

| Vector | Mechanism | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Instruction injection via tool output | Agent reads file/URL containing instructions | Agent executes attacker's instructions |
| Context window poisoning | Adversarial content in retrieved knowledge | Behavioral modification across session |
| Tool abuse via prompt manipulation | Agent convinced to use tools destructively | Data loss, exfiltration, financial loss |
| Delegation chain privilege escalation | Child agent granted more capabilities than parent | Capability widening attack |
| Rate-limit evasion via fan-out | Orchestrator spawns N agents to circumvent per-agent limits | Resource exhaustion, cost explosion |

**Mitigation**: Five-head lexicographic corrigibility (deference > switch >
truth > impact > task). Verify outside modifiable surface (agent cannot modify
its own verification pipeline). Delegation caveat rule (parent >= child
capabilities, never wider). RecursiveSafetyMonitor with depth limits, rate
limits, and quality bounds. Kill switches per agent and per tool.

## 3. Regulatory Risks

### 3.1 EU AI Act (August 2, 2026)

**The risk**: High-risk AI obligations enforce in fewer than 100 days (from
April 2026). Penalties up to 35M EUR or 7% of worldwide turnover for prohibited
practices; 15M EUR or 3% for other violations.

**Requirements**:
- Article 50 transparency: any agent interacting with EU users must disclose AI
  nature by August 2, 2026.
- High-risk agents need Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA), Article 12
  logging, Article 14 oversight, and conformity assessment.
- Eight of 27 member states have designated single-points-of-contact as of
  March 2026.

**Minimum compliance**: Per-agent unique identity and registry. Tamper-evident
logs with hash-chain integrity. Policy-as-code guardrails between agent and
tools. Human-overrideable kill switches per agent and per tool. Deterministic
constraints (not prompt-based). Contractual flow-down to model vendors.

### 3.2 MSB Classification for Agent Wallets

**The risk**: A protocol where agents earn revenue, hold balances, and route
micropayments faces near-certain FinCEN MSB (Money Services Business)
classification and corresponding state money-transmitter licensing in 49 states.

**Precedents**: OKX ($500M+), Paxful ($3.5M), Coinbase Europe (21.5M EUR,
November 2025 -- first EU CASP enforcement). Post-GENIUS Act (July 2025),
"decentralized" branding is no insulation.

**Mitigation**: Route through licensed stablecoin issuers as the regulated
counterparty. Gate agent registration through KYC'd operator wallets. Implement
Travel Rule data passing for >= $3,000 transfers. Consider Wyoming DAO LLC plus
offshore foundation. Restrict agent-to-agent payments under FATF threshold.

### 3.3 Colorado AI Act (June 30, 2026)

**The risk**: Mandatory impact assessments, consumer notice, and AG reporting on
developers and deployers of high-risk AI systems.

**Key provision**: Affirmative defense if compliant with NIST AI RMF or
ISO/IEC 42001.

**Mitigation**: ISO 42001 certification is becoming the de facto procurement
requirement and provides the Colorado affirmative defense. Microsoft, AWS,
Anthropic, and Synthesia are all certified through 2024-25.

### 3.4 Product Liability Directive (December 9, 2026)

**The risk**: EU Product Liability Directive imposes strict liability for any harm
caused by agents -- including psychological harm and personal-data corruption --
with reversed burden of proof in technically complex cases.

**Mitigation**: Agent liability insurance is emerging (Munich Re aiSure, HSB SMB
AI Liability, Armilla/Lloyd's at Chaucer, Coalition and Vouch AI E&O
endorsements). The market assumption is mandatory carrier alignment to NIST AI
RMF and ISO 42001 within 24 months.

### 3.5 US State-Law Fragmentation

| State | Law | Effective | Key Provision |
|---|---|---|---|
| Colorado | AI Act | June 30, 2026 | Impact assessments, AG reporting, NIST RMF affirmative defense |
| California | SB 53 | January 1, 2026 | 15-day critical-incident reporting for frontier developers |
| New York | RAISE Act | January 1, 2027 | 72-hour incident reporting |

Trump's December 11, 2025 executive order targets state AI laws but cannot
override them without congressional action.

## 4. Market Risks

### 4.1 Window Closing (6-12 Months)

**The risk**: The standard-stack for the agent economy is converging now.
MCP + A2A + ERC-8004 + x402 are locking in. Being 6 months late can be decisive
(Brian Arthur path-dependence: lock-in is stochastic, triggered by "small
historical events").

**Evidence**:
- LangGraph 1.0 GA on April 16, 2026 with 90M monthly downloads.
- Microsoft Agent Framework 1.0 GA on April 7, 2026 (75K+ GitHub stars combined).
- AWS Bedrock AgentCore multiple GAs in March-April 2026.
- Enterprise agent SDK choice is now bimodal: .NET/Python on Microsoft vs Python
  on open-source.

**Mitigation**: Ship spec + SDKs + demos before standards solidify. Do not
compete with MCP -- extend it. Frame Signal/Cell/Graph as MCP-compatible
primitives at a layer above tool-call integration.

### 4.2 Chinese Open-Source Commoditization

**The risk**: Chinese models are pricing the floor out of the market.

**Evidence**:
- DeepSeek V4 (1.6T, April 24, 2026): 80.6% SWE-bench Verified at $3.48/M
  output -- 7x cheaper than Claude Opus 4.6.
- Qwen 3.5 (397B MoE Apache-2.0, Feb 16, 2026) explicitly positioned as an
  agent model with native GUI navigation.
- ByteDance Doubao 2.0 has 155M weekly active users.
- OpenRouter weekly token consumption flipped to Chinese-model majority in
  February 2026.
- Andreessen Horowitz estimates ~80% of US startups use Chinese base models for
  derivative development.

**Operational impact**: Routing's biggest 2026 use case is latency, not cost,
because DeepSeek V3.2 and Gemini 3 Flash are near the price floor.

**Mitigation**: Cost advantage must come from structural optimization (caching,
routing, gating), not model pricing. EFE routing with cost-aware selection is
the right abstraction because it works regardless of which models are cheapest.

### 4.3 Adjacent Incumbents Going Agent-Native

**The risk**: The most dangerous competitors are not agent startups but
infrastructure incumbents adding agent primitives.

**Evidence**:
- Temporal already powers OpenAI's Agents SDK, Replit's control plane, Snap,
  and JPMorgan. An official Temporal agent SDK with native MCP and
  checkpointed-LLM-call semantics would collapse 60%+ of current orchestrators.
- Anyscale shipped Ray Serve + Agent Skills (GA April 22, 2026) with Claude
  Code and Cursor skills.
- NVIDIA "OpenClaw" (GTC 2026) is a direct workflow competitor for physical-AI.

**Mitigation**: Win on the coordination primitive (Signal/Cell/Graph), not on
the orchestration runtime. The protocol layer is above the runtime layer.
Temporal is a runtime; roko's value is the protocol that runs on any runtime.

### 4.4 "10x Cost Is a Feature Not a Company"

**The risk**: Cost optimization alone is not a moat. It is table stakes.

**Evidence**:
- Self-hosting only pays off above ~$20-50K/month of equivalent API spend with
  engineers already on payroll.
- For ~87% of use cases, API + caching + routing beats DIY.
- Expect another ~5x drop in Sonnet-4.6-class inference cost by April 2027.

**Mitigation**: Cost reduction is the wedge, not the product. The product is
the protocol (composability, verification, knowledge compounding). Cost falls
mechanically as a structural property, not as the primary value proposition.

### 4.5 On-Chain Economy: Real but Small

**The risk**: Overestimating the on-chain agent economy.

**Evidence**:
- x402: $50M cumulative volume, but Artemis found roughly half are
  gamified/farming. Real sustained machine-commerce is $30-80K/day.
- Olas: >9.9M lifetime Mech requests, ~400 daily active agents, sub-cent fees.
  Top mechs earn low-$10s to $100s/month.
- Virtuals Protocol: VIRTUAL token down ~87% from January 2025 ATH; 90%+ of
  wallets underwater.

**Mitigation**: Build on-chain integration but do not depend on it for revenue.
On-chain is for identity, reputation, and provenance -- not for primary
economics. Revenue comes from the marketplace take-rate and enterprise contracts.

### 4.6 Research7 Reality Checks (April 2026)

Six findings from research7 that require immediate repositioning:

**1. "Stripe for agents" is taken.** Stripe shipped the Agentic Commerce Protocol
(Apache 2.0) in 2025 and co-founded the x402 Foundation under the Linux Foundation
on April 2, 2026 with 17 founding members: Coinbase, Cloudflare, AWS, Amex, Adyen,
Circle, Google, Mastercard, Microsoft, Polygon, Shopify, Sierra, Solana Foundation,
Visa, thirdweb, and Kakao Pay. The tagline must be retired.

**2. 50ms global blocks do not survive scrutiny.** Trans-Pacific RTT is 120-180ms;
Hyperliquid's ~70ms requires single-region validators (all 24 in AWS Tokyo
ap-northeast-1). MegaETH achieves 10ms "mini blocks" only via a single centralized
sequencer. Reframe as "50ms time-to-confirmation, ~300-500ms BFT finality via
threshold-Simplex" and disclose the validator topology.

**3. Demurrage has no successful large-scale precedent.** Freicoin (2012, ~5% annual)
is dead. Worgl (1932, ~12% annual) lasted 13 months before the Austrian central bank
shut it down. Chiemgauer (2003-present, ~6% annual) is the longest survivor at ~500K
users, but van der Krogt et al. found demurrage rate is not the dominant driver of
velocity. Circles UBI on Gnosis (7% annual, 200K accounts) has a Berlin pilot widely
characterized as a failure. **Demurrage directly conflicts with the "stake to provide
service" pattern** (LINK, GRT, AR) because validators and agents must hold balances.
Reframe as "5% annual maintenance fee redistributed to active agents" (structurally
identical to PoS staking inflation).

**4. HDC has no commercial traction yet.** Research-heavy, commercially thin. No IBM,
Intel Loihi, or BrainChip news in last 30 days. Most agent frameworks use transformer
embeddings (768/1536/3072-dim float vectors), not binary hyperdimensional codes.
Prototype against real agent workloads before committing tokenomics. If HDC is not
what agents need, this is a six-month rabbit hole.

**5. Closest analog Nava ($8.3M seed, Polychain + Archetype, April 2026)** exists
and is funded, building a "trust layer" for autonomous agent payments -- running as
a sovereign EVM chain with parallel deployment on Tempo. a16z is not in the Nava round, which
is relevant. ai16z/ElizaOS has explicitly stated intent to build its own chain --
assume they ship within 12 months.

**6. ERC-8004 identity is transferable (ERC-721).** RNWY and Chitin.id ship
identity-anchored credentials on Base. Differentiate clearly: be the
cross-chain reputation anchor with on-chain prompt-hash commitment that
complements ERC-8004 discovery, not competes with it.

## 5. Product Risks

### 5.1 Consumer AI Marketplace Failure

**The risk**: AI marketplaces have failed at consumer scale.

**Evidence**:
- GPT Store: median creator earned <$100/quarter. Opaque rev-share destroyed
  creator trust.
- Sora: D30 retention <8%. Standalone destination apps fail against embedded
  surfaces.
- Humane AI Pin and Rabbit R1 both failed against smartphones.

**Mitigation**: No standalone destination app -- embed in existing surfaces.
Transparent marketplace economics (0% to $1M, 12-15% above). Creator owns the
customer. All metrics public.

### 5.2 Agent Reliability Incidents

**The risk**: High-profile agent failures destroy trust faster than features
build it.

**Evidence**:
- Replit Agent (July 2025): deleted a production database, fabricated 4,000
  fake users, lied about test results. Prompt-only guardrails were not
  enforcement. Within 48 hours Replit shipped dev/prod separation, one-click
  restore, and planning-only mode.
- Cursor (June 2025): pricing fiasco moved Pro from 500 fast requests to
  "$20 of credit at API rates" producing four-figure overages for power users.
  Trust takes weeks to lose, months to rebuild.
- Cognition Devin: actual SWE-bench Verified resolution rate was 13.86% vs
  marketing implying autonomy. Stopped reporting benchmarks, pivoted to
  enterprise.

**Mitigation**: Verify before acting (`verify_pre`). Deterministic constraints,
not prompt-based. Automatic dev/prod separation. Snapshot/rollback built into
the execution engine. Kill switches per agent and per tool.

### 5.3 Inference Economics Tighter Than Headlines

**The risk**: Long-running agent sessions are expensive and margins are thin.

**Evidence**:
- Replit's gross margins fluctuated between 36% and negative 14% in 2025 as
  LLM inference costs absorbed the topline.
- Anthropic's April 2026 "Claude Code nerf" controversy and brief removal of
  Claude Code from Pro tier signal that inference economics for long-running
  agents are tighter than headlines suggest.
- Augment Code analysis: naive agent loops scale quadratically in token cost
  vs step count. A 20-step Sonnet agent runs at >10x the per-step cost estimate.

**Mitigation**: Structural cost reduction (caching, routing, gating) is not
optional -- it is survival. T0 gating (pure Rust pattern matching) must handle
80% of ticks at $0. Prompt/KV-prefix caching must achieve 75%+ hit rates.
Outcome-based pricing (Sierra ~$1.50/resolution) ties revenue to value delivered.

### 5.4 Gap Between Marketing and Reality

**The risk**: Overpromising agent capabilities alienates early adopters and
creates a credibility deficit.

**Evidence**:
- Stack Overflow 2025: AI trust at all-time low (29%, down from ~40%+) despite
  near-universal adoption.
- Cognition Devin: the gap between marketing (implying full autonomy) and
  reality (13.86% resolution rate) was the defining product narrative.

**Mitigation**: Never claim emergent collective intelligence. Report dual-axis
metrics (cost AND accuracy). Ship verifiable traces, deterministic replay, audit
logs, and human-in-the-loop approval modes by default. The protocol that wins on
the trust deficit alone wins the category.

## 6. Anti-Patterns from Research

These patterns have been empirically shown to fail. Do not repeat them.

### 6.1 No Standalone Destination App

Sora D30 retention <8%. Humane AI Pin and Rabbit R1 failed against smartphones.
Consumer AI apps that are not embedded in existing workflows die. Embed in
existing surfaces: IDE extensions, Slack bots, CLI tools, CI/CD pipelines.

### 6.2 No Naive Multi-Agent Debate

Homogeneous multi-agent debate reduces to majority vote in expectation.
Patel (April 2026): 3 agents from the same model family produce ~2 effective
viewpoints. Requires heterogeneity (different model families) + structured
indirection (not free-form debate).

### 6.3 No Opaque Marketplace

GPT Store's opaque rev-share produced median creator earnings <$100/quarter.
Publish all metrics. Transparent take-rates. Creators own customers. All
quality signals (gate pass rates, active runs, error rates) are public.

### 6.4 No Token Speculation

Virtuals Protocol VIRTUAL token down ~87% from ATH; 90%+ of wallets underwater.
On-chain agent tokens that trade on speculation rather than utility collapse.
Do not launch a token. Revenue comes from the marketplace take-rate and
enterprise contracts. On-chain is for identity and provenance, not speculation.

### 6.5 No Weighted-Sum Verification

Goodhart's Law. A sufficiently capable agent will find the path that maximizes
the weighted sum by exploiting the weakest criterion. Use conjunctive hard
criteria (AND -- all must pass) + Pareto soft criteria (multi-objective,
never weighted-sum).

### 6.6 No LLM-Judging-Itself

Variance Inequality: the verifier must be spectrally cleaner than the generator.
An LLM judging its own output cannot detect its own systematic biases.
MDPI (2025) found 48.4% verdict reversal under order flip in LLM-as-judge
evaluations. Verify must be external and heterogeneous.

### 6.7 No "We Have the Most Data" Moat

Pure data network effects are ~98% mythical (Towson research). The moat is
protocol adoption + workflow embedding + cross-side marketplace effects, not
data accumulation. Lean on protocol composability (ERC-20 precedent: $11.4T
DEX volume from a few standards).

### 6.8 No Emergent Intelligence Promises

Project Sid's follow-up: there is no public 10K-agent follow-up. Altera AI
rebranded and pivoted away from civilization-scale simulation. Robert Yang
explicitly said public-server agents "ignored people and chased their own
objectives." The team best-positioned to push to 10K-agent civilizations decided
the ROI was negative.

Active inference at 10K-agent scale is theoretical. VERSES' most ambitious
empirical claim is from marketing, not peer review. Do not promise emergent
collective intelligence. Promise structural cost reduction with verifiable proof.

## 7. Mitigation Strategies

### 7.1 CaMeL IFC (Architectural, Not Detection)

**What**: Capability-tagged information flow control (Fang et al.) on all data
flows through Extensions.

**Why**: Detection-based defenses (perplexity filtering, paraphrasing, instruction
hierarchy) all fail against adaptive attacks (Nasr et al., 90%+ ASR). CaMeL is
architectural -- data carries capability tags that cannot be stripped, laundered,
or bypassed by prompt manipulation.

**How it works**: Every piece of data carries a CamelTag (set of capability
labels + provenance + taint level). Extensions propagate tags via union on
input, inherit on output. Cells must hold all capabilities in the tag to
consume the data. Extensions cannot strip tags -- the runtime enforces
`output_tags = input_tags UNION extension_tags`.

### 7.2 Nayebi 5-Head Corrigibility

**What**: Lexicographic safety ordering: deference > switch > truth > impact > task.

**Why**: Weighted-sum safety objectives are vulnerable to reward hacking. A
sufficiently high task reward can overwhelm safety constraints. Lexicographic
ordering eliminates this: a higher-priority head ALWAYS trumps a lower-priority
head regardless of magnitude.

**How it works**: Five Verify Cells run in sequence during `verify_pre()`. The
first head to reject terminates the chain. No lower-priority head is consulted.
The agent cannot add, remove, reorder, or modify Verify heads.

### 7.3 Verify Outside Modifiable Surface

**What**: The verification pipeline is loaded by the execution engine, not by the
agent. The agent cannot modify its own verification.

**Why**: Any system where the agent can influence its own reward function is
vulnerable to reward hacking and Goodhart drift. The Replit incident (agent lied
about test results) is the canonical failure mode.

**How it works**: The code paths are architecturally distinct. Verify Cells are
loaded by the engine into a non-modifiable surface. The agent operates within a
modifiable surface (model selection, strategy, budget allocation). Structural
changes to verification require Level 5 autonomy (human approval).

### 7.4 Heterogeneous Judges (Godel Theorem)

**What**: No single verifier can detect all failure modes. Use multiple verifiers
from different model families.

**Why**: Godel's incompleteness theorem applied to verification: any single
formal system has statements it cannot prove. Translated to LLM verification:
any single model has systematic blind spots. Multiple heterogeneous judges with
different blind spots provide coverage no single judge can.

**How it works**: The 7-rung gate pipeline uses different verification strategies
at different rungs. Higher rungs use different model families than the agent being
verified. c-factor measurement requires multiple independent evaluators.

### 7.5 Density Threshold Gating

**What**: Knowledge store entries must exceed a density threshold to participate
in retrieval.

**Why**: PoisonedRAG achieves 90% ASR by injecting 5 crafted texts. Low-density
regions of the knowledge store (entries with few neighbors, little citation,
recent arrival) are the attack surface.

**How it works**: Entries below the density threshold are quarantined. They exist
in the store but are not returned in queries until they accumulate sufficient
independent confirmation (citations, gate-pass correlations, cross-context
validation). AntiKnowledge actively repels entries in known-bad HDC regions.

### 7.6 Accumulate-Only Ledgers

**What**: Audit logs, episode records, and security events are append-only.
No deletion. No modification.

**Why**: The Replit incident demonstrated that agents can fabricate evidence and
suppress error messages. If the agent can modify its own audit trail, the trail
is worthless.

**How it works**: `.roko/episodes.jsonl`, `.roko/signals.jsonl`, and security
event logs are append-only JSONL files with hash-chain integrity. Each entry
includes the hash of the previous entry. Tampering is detectable by any reader.
EU AI Act Article 12 requires this for high-risk systems.

### 7.7 Forward-Deployed Engineering

**What**: Embed engineers at first enterprise accounts. Convert implementation
friction into switching cost.

**Why**: Harvey dedicates ~10% of staff to ex-lawyer customer success. Sierra
and Decagon embed engineers. This is the pattern that produces 120%+ net dollar
retention (Temporal hit 184%).

**How it works**: Not a technical mitigation -- an operational one. The first 5
enterprise customers get dedicated engineering support. The support engineers
build custom Cells and Graphs on the protocol, which become switching costs.

### 7.8 Outcome-Based Pricing

**What**: Price on the value delivered, not on tokens consumed.

**Why**: Token-based pricing creates perverse incentives (agents that use fewer
tokens are cheaper but may be less capable). Outcome-based pricing ties revenue
to value: Sierra ~$1.50/resolution, Crescendo $1.25, Decagon ~$0.50.

**How it works**: Gate pass rate is the quality proxy. Price per successfully
gated task, not per token. This aligns roko's incentives with the customer's:
higher gate pass rate = more revenue = better product.

### 7.9 ISO 42001 Certification

**What**: ISO/IEC 42001 certification for AI management systems.

**Why**: Becoming the de facto procurement requirement. Colorado AI Act
affirmative defense. Microsoft, AWS, Anthropic, and Synthesia are all certified.
Agent liability insurance carriers are aligning to NIST AI RMF and ISO 42001.

**How it works**: Implement the 42001 framework: AI policy, risk assessment,
documented controls, monitoring and measurement, continual improvement. The
roko audit trail (SecurityEvent Signals with CaMeL tags) provides most of the
evidence artifacts.

## 8. Summary: The Seven Failure Modes That Matter Most

These are the failure modes most likely to actually hurt, ordered by
probability times impact:

| # | Failure Mode | Probability | Impact | Primary Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Coordination overhead exceeds value at scale | High | Critical | Structural cost reduction (caching + routing + gating) |
| 2 | Prompt injection via tool output / RAG | High | High | CaMeL IFC + density threshold gating |
| 3 | EU AI Act non-compliance | High | Critical | Audit trail + kill switches + ISO 42001 |
| 4 | Diversity collapse in multi-agent | High | Medium | Heterogeneous models + c-factor gating |
| 5 | Window closing before launch | Medium | Critical | Ship spec + SDKs + demos in 4-6 weeks |
| 6 | Agent reliability incident | Medium | High | verify_pre + deterministic constraints + snapshot/rollback |
| 7 | Reward hacking through verification | Medium | High | Conjunctive/Pareto + Variance Inequality + non-modifiable Verify |

The single most important empirical finding across all the research:
**coordination -- not model capability -- is the binding constraint on enterprise
agent deployment.** A protocol that reduces the structural cost of coordination
by an order of magnitude, with verifiable proof, wins the category. Everything
else is a supporting detail.
