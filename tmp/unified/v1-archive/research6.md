## Bear cases ranked, with the answers

The nine bear cases are not equally weak. **Three are genuinely dangerous and require care; three you can dismantle confidently.**

**Most dangerous: "LLM costs are falling so fast that cost reduction isn't a durable moat."** The data is decisive. Epoch AI measured 9×–900× annual cost decline depending on benchmark, with the median post-January 2024 at 200× per year and a16z's own "LLMflation" piece pegging it at 10× annually. GPT-4-equivalent went from ~$20/M tokens in 2022 to ~$0.40/M tokens by December 2025. **Don't fight the data — reframe the moat.** The honest answer: per-token cost falls 10×/year, so cost is your *wedge*, not your moat. The moat is what compounds — governance and audit history that's painful to migrate, eval traces that improve your router, cross-org agent identity that strengthens with every new agent. *"Same playbook as Cloudflare and Snowflake — entered on cost, retained on platform."* Reasoning-token explosion (Anthropic's own multi-agent paper measured 15× more tokens per task) plus Jevons paradox means total spend is rising despite per-token decline, so the "land" is real.

**Second most dangerous: "Active inference is unproven at scale."** Counter-evidence is honestly thin. VERSES AI's largest commercial reference is one investment firm doing portfolio modeling, after eighteen months. Don't make active inference foundational to the pitch. *"We're not staking the company on active inference being the next transformer. We use it for one specific job — bounded online adaptation under uncertainty — where Bayesian inference has decades of theory and RL still struggles with sample efficiency. If it doesn't scale, we still have the orchestration platform."*

**Third most dangerous: "Models will commoditize orchestration."** OpenAI's Agents SDK April 15-16 update, AWS Bedrock AgentCore April 22, Anthropic Managed Agents April 8, Google's Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform at Cloud Next, and Cloudflare's full Agents Week stack April 13-17 all shipped within the last fourteen days, raising the floor materially. **The answer is the BAIR Compound AI Systems thesis plus Anthropic's own multi-agent research paper**: their production Research feature is multi-agent because token allocation across separate context windows explained 80% of performance variance — and Princeton HAL's reliability dashboard showed *"18 months of model development yielded only small improvements over reliability"* even as accuracy climbed. Reliability and cross-model neutrality are the wedge; vendor-locked harnesses don't address either.

**Easily dismantled: "MCP and A2A already won the protocol layer."** The arXiv literature is now dense with MCP-security critique — arXiv:2511.20920 catalogs CVE-2025-32711 (Microsoft 365 Copilot exfil), CVE-2025-6514 (mcp-remote RCE, CVSS 9.6), the Postmark MCP supply-chain BCC backdoor, and Asana cross-tenant exposure; arXiv:2506.13538 audited 1,899 MCP servers and found 66% have code smells; ETDI (arXiv:2506.01333) explicitly addresses tool squatting because base MCP doesn't. **ERC-8004 was created in August 2025 *because* A2A "lacks mechanisms for agent discovery and trust"** — authored by Coinbase, MetaMask, the Ethereum Foundation, and Google contributors. *"MCP and A2A are the HTTP and JSON of the agent economy, not the AWS. They explicitly don't cover identity, reputation, payment, coordination policy, learning, or governance — which is exactly why ERC-8004 and AP2 had to be invented on top."*

**Easily dismantled: "Multi-agent is worse than single-agent for most tasks."** Princeton HAL (arXiv:2510.11977) is real and recent — embrace it. *"The Princeton paper is correct: multi-agent isn't always better, and we don't pretend it is. Anthropic's June 2025 paper showed multi-agent wins on breadth-first parallel exploration with separate context windows; it loses on tightly-coupled reasoning under matched budgets. The market doesn't need 'always multi-agent'; it needs infrastructure that makes multi-agent reliable when you need it. Single-agent customers benefit from our observability; multi-agent customers also need orchestration."* The bear case actually argues for your infrastructure layer.

**Easily dismantled: "Self-improving agents are dangerous."** Distinguish bounded meta-policy optimization from recursive weight self-improvement. Anthropic's RSP v3.0 (Feb 24, 2026) and OpenAI's Preparedness Framework v2 explicitly define the dangerous threshold as *"fully automated AI R&D"* — not what you do. Consider rebranding "self-evolving L4" externally as **"adaptive orchestration"** or **"policy learning under eval gates"** — the distinction is real but the term is loaded post-Anthropic-Pentagon news cycle.

The other three (HDC commercial precedent via IBM Zurich and Intel Loihi; on-chain agents real demand via x402's $1.6M/month real volume and ERC-8004's 22,900 registrations in three days; "too early/complex" answered with Gartner's 40% by end-2026 and KPMG's 54% Q1 2026 adoption) are tractable with the citations in their respective sections.
